Preface: My Connection to these Recipes

I was born in Tehran in a big hospital on a snowy day and so, I was born directly into the heart of Persian Culture. It never managed to rub off on me nearly as much as it should have, however. I stayed in the country until I was 7, which was when me, my mom, and my dad all immigrated to Toronto which brings us here. I have such fond memories back there of fooling around playing imaginary games with my Cousin at my Grandparents’ house, and of exploring the mountains and going to restaurants. It brings back memories of people; some of which, I only have memories left of. But the Culture never rubbed off on me as much as it should have. I learned English alongside Farsi since Kindergarten, and I remember the exact moment I took my seat on the plane to Canada, the first thought that ran through my head was “I think I completely forgot how to read Farsi…” And so it was. Even now, I don’t know how to read or write Farsi, and I know little of how to speak it and my vocabulary is as minimal as can be. Therefore, one of the main things that ties me back to the country I come from is food! My mom blesses me on a daily basis by making me all the foods from her, and now from my childhood, and it’s the main thing I have to relive those days through. It’s what’s normal for me. These recipes are what I’m used to, and what I prefer. I don’t know a few of the recipes in this cookbook, and I won’t pretend like I’ve made all of these and that I’m some cooking professional. These are a sample of the flavours from where I come from. Many of them are my favourite flavours, too. Nothing beats Some Koobideh Kebab on a sunny day just how I like it, and you, my wondrous reader, will now get a glimpse into many of these dishes. I hope I inspire you to try some of them for yourself, and I most certainly hope you find something to enjoy in this endeavour. Without further ado…

The Equipment You’ll Need

There are few recipes in this Cookbook that demand more than the basic things you’re likely to already have. Pans, Pots, Bowls, Whisks, Spatulas, Cups, and Sieves, for instance. You will find, however, that some recipes differ in this sense:

An extra large Cooking Pot

Aush Reshteh is one food that is served nearly exclusively on festive occasions and, by design, comes in incredibly large quantities. Because of this, you’ll want to grab the biggest pot that can still fit on your stove that you can find. I recall from the one time I was able to see it being made, it was in my Grandparents’ basement in a pot so big it reached from the floor up to my Uncle’s knees, and of course that’s comically large and perhaps excessive so just generally know you’ll want to find a bigger than normal brewing pot for Aush Reshteh.

Heat Diffuser

This is technically optional, but is heavily recommended for Khagineh. Heat Diffusers ensure equal distribution of heat in a Pot, and Khagineh is supposed to be made in a very unique manner wherein it’s fried inside a pot. Because of this; for ease of use, a Heat Diffuser is heavily recommended.

Skewers and a Grill

Kebab Koobideh is supposed to be made on a grill. It’s in the name, in fact: Koobideh means Pounded, which refers to the fact that the meat is supposed to be pounded onto the Skewers and then put over a grill. As with Aush, it’s a dish that takes a lot of effort to create authentically, and so is usually reserved for parties and gatherings such as Picnics.

Breakfasts

Fereni

Figure 1: A small bowl of Fereni with some pistachio pieces and a little rose bloom sprinkled over top

When I was much younger, Fereni was one of my favourite breakfasts/snacks, and I cherished it every time my mom or my grandma made me some. Fereni is nothing more than a fairly simple little Pudding, in truth, but it will always hold a special place in my heart, and I hope it serves as a convenient light little addition to your breakfast!

Time Needed

  • 10m Prep Time
  • 10m Cook Time
  • 20m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 2/3 cup cornstarch (y’know, it always manages to be corn; corn is actually everywhere when you think about it)
  • 2 cups milk (yeah, sorry Vegans, milk is kinda the main ingredient at play here…)
  • 6 whole cardamom seeds
  • 1/2 cup ground almonds
  • 1/2 cup Rosewater (well, add to taste actually; I personally prefer ~1/2 cup or so)
  • 1/4 cup white sugar (alternatively add to taste, but definitely don’t add too much; Fereni is sickening when it’s too sweet)
  • 1/4 cup blanched, slivered almonds (I wonder if Walnut may work better, actually; up to you, reader)

Directions

  1. Dissolve your cornstarch in 1 cup of cold milk
  2. Bring out the other cup of milk, and boil it; making sure to add in the cardamom seeds and ground almonds. After reaching a boil, reduce the heat to medium, and whisk in your cornstarch mixture from before
  3. Pour in your selected quantities of sugar and rosewater. Allow this mixture to finally boil for about an additional 3m, on medium heat whilst constantly stirring. Remove the cardamom seeds from the mixture, and pour into serving dishes
  4. Finally, simply garnish with slivered almonds or walnuts, and serve. Fereni can be enjoyed both hot and cold. Enjoy!

Shir-Berenj (Rice Pudding)

Figure 2: A bowl of Shir-Berenj with some Pistachio pieces and raisins as garnish (we love our pistachios)

Shir-Berenj (better known as Rice Pudding, or to take the literal translation: Milk-Rice) is an incredibly straightforward and simple food, but serves as a delicious way to start even the worst of mornings. The way I had it growing up made it simple for my mom to make, but I loved it all the same. I’m sure you will too… Seeing how it’s impossible to not like Rice Pudding! You may notice how it sounds quite similar to Fereni (or maybe that’s just me) but I assure you, the two are quite unique.

Time Needed

  • 5m Prep Time
  • 3h40m Cook Time
  • 3h45m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 1 cup short grain White Rice
  • 1/2 tsp Table Salt
  • 4 cups whole milk (Once again, I’m so sorry Vegans; alternatives to milk can likely be experimented with here)
  • 2 cups Heavy Cream (The quantity can vary depending on how thick you want your Shir-Berenj)
  • 2 cups Water
  • 1/4 cup Rosewater (fun fact: in Farsi, Rosewater is golab which is very similar to the Farsi word for Cherry too)
  • 1 tsp Cinnamon
  • 1 cup White Sugar
  • ~2 tsps Warm Delicious Honey (optional, technically, but Rice Pudding and Honey just play well)

Directions

  1. Rinse your rice with those 2 cups of room temperature water; this should take at least 2h
  2. Pour your rice into a pot on low-medium heat and add some Salt. Cook your rice until the grains begin falling apart from one another
  3. Add in your milk and heavy cream, then bring the concoction to a boil
  4. Add sugar and Cardamom and begin mixing
  5. Bring the heat down to low and cook for about 1h until the pudding thickens. Once this is done, you’re done! Now just ladle the Shir-Berenj into cups or small bowls and top with garnishes of your choice (I like to simply use plain Honey)

Adasi (Lentil Soup)

Figure 1: A large bowl of Adasi; our chunky, hearty, Lentil and Potato Soup

Adasi is essentially just the Persian spin on Lentil Soup, and it’s not something I liked very much growing up. I found the taste to be acceptable, but it was more so the combination with little lentils you have to chew on and the liquid of the soup that made it unbearable for me. Texture is important, man, what can I say? When we tried it earlier last year in Food and Nutrition class, funnily enough actually, I discovered that I like it now. I hope you’ll like this recipe just the same!

Time Needed

  • 10m Prep Time
  • 40m Cook Time
  • 50m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 1 cup Lentils (This may come as a surprise, but these are pretty important in this recipe)
  • 3 cups Water
  • 1 Onion (Colour doesn’t really matter but I’d recommend White Onion)
  • 1 Potato (We usually just use a plain old Russett Potato)
  • Variable quantity of Oil (Use as much as needed; I’d probably be scolded if I recommended anything except Olive Oil)
  • Variable quantities of Salt, Pepper, Cinnamon (optional), and Turmeric (Use as much as needed)

Directions

  1. 4h prior to cooking the Adasi, leave your Lentils in a bowl of room temperature water so they absorb it and grow big and strong
  2. Pour your tasty little Lentil children into a pot in some water, and apply Medium Heat! Chop your Potato into semi-thick slices, and when the Lentils are half cooked, add them to the pot
  3. Chop up the Onions then stir-fry them in a pan (you can do this above a separate source of heat as the pot, but for the sake of convenience I’d highly recommend this new handy dandy invention I came across in the Flea Market a few weeks ago called the Stove) with some Oil and Turmeric. Finally, add a dash of Cinnamon (don’t) to the Onions
  4. After your Potatoes have fully baked, remove around half of them from the pot and mash them. Now, add the mashed potatoes to your Onions. Stir it up, and finally add the whole thing to your still cooking Lentils
  5. Add some salt and pepper, and let it cook for ten more minutes.
  6. Enjoy!

Haleem

Figure 1: A bowl of Haleem with a cross of Cinnamon (not affiliated with Christianity) and seeds over top

Haleem is a dish that always seemed to me like it would be delicious, but I was always also skittish at the thought of there being Lamb inside of a soup which is so obviously supposed to be sweet. I also mixed it up with Halva during the making of this Cookbook so that’s nice. It isn’t something I’ve had yet, but I’m hoping that’ll change someday rather soon!

Time Needed

  • 3h Prep Time (Woah)
  • 4h Cook Time
  • 7h Total Time

Ingredients

  • 300g Peeled Wheat (Man, I was wondering when Wheat was going to make an appearance!)
  • Small amount of Oil (This will get poured over top some water; the amount doesn’t need to be precise, and I’d recommend just standard Vegetable Oil)
  • 300g Mutton (Feel free to also just simply not add the mutton; doesn’t taste like it’ll go so well anyways frankly)
  • 1 tsp Salt
  • Some small quantity of Butter (This will be used to optionally garnish the Haleem so go with your own preferred quantity, Reader)
  • To taste quantity of Cinnamon
  • To taste quantity of Sugar
  • To taste quantity of Sesame
  • To taste quantity of Almond
  • To taste quantity of Dried Damask Rose

Directions

  1. After washing your Wheat (hah, sick rhymes) leave it in some warm water for about an entire day (preferably think ahead when making Haleem, therefore)
  2. Use from fresh water to cook the wheat in a pot; start with a boil, then turn the temperature down from there. Pour a little bit of Oil over this, and be sure to regularly stir. Keep cooking the wheat until it becomes mashed (about 4 hours) and finally use a sieve and separate the remaining pieces of the skin from the wheat itself
  3. Chop an Onion into quarters, and cook your Lamb and the Onions all in another Pot using two cups of Water (If you don’t have 2 Pots then… Sorry) and optionally add some Cinnamon
  4. After the wheat is finished, add the Lamb and salt to it. Use a piece of cloth as an infuser on top of the pot and then cover the pot with it’s Lid (the same thing is done with Loobia Polo) and make sure to stir the Mutton regularly to prevent burning
  5. Plate your Haleem (or more sensibly, bowl it) and garnish (optionally) with Sesame, Almond Slices, Dried Damask Rose, Sugar, and Cinnamon, and also your Butter of course!
  6. Enjoy!

Lunches

Adas Polo

Figure 1: A plate of Adas Polo with some white and yellow rice and raisins (Please don’t actually include raisins they are my mortal enemy)

Adas Polo is an absolute classic for me. It’s what I would have for lunch essentially at least once a week, and I still eat it very often! I like my Adas Polo rather plain, but well, what can I say? I happen to still have a portion of my sanity left. Raisins, dates, and Walnuts have no place in Adas Polo in my opinion. Regardless, Adas Polo is a dish that can be made as a lunch for a common day, or to treat an entire party. Whatever the case, it’s always delicious!

Time Needed

  • 10m Prep Time
  • 1h15m Cook Time
  • 1h25m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 1 cup Lentils (Adas is the word for Lentils; they are the main ingredient)
  • 200g Ground Beef (Technically optional, but Adas Polo is very plain without Ground Beef in my own opinion)
  • 2 tbsps Vegetable Oil
  • 1 whole Onion; finely chopped
  • 1 tsp Turmeric (Mmmmmmmm mighty Arabian Spices)
  • 1/2 tsp Cinnamon (I would omit using this personally; you can if you’d like to but it makes the dish feel too chalky to me)
  • 3 tsp Salt
  • 2 pinches Saffron Threads (This is pretty much the Persian spice)
  • 1 cup Raisins (Optional; please just do yourself a favour and skip this)
  • 1 cup Pitted Mejdool Dates (Optional too; Same as with the Raisins, please do not add these for your own sake)
  • 2 cups Basmati Rice

Directions

  1. Wash and drain your rice well if you haven’t already; just rinse until the water runs clear
  2. Rinse the Lentils with cold water, and leave them to soak for about 1-2h to speed up the cooking process later
  3. Wash your raisins - if you decided to use raisins - and pat them dry on Kitchen Paper
  4. Add your lentils to a saucepan, with a ratio of 3 cups of water for every 1 cup of lentils. Bring this to a boil and then apply a very tight cover, and reduce the heat to simmer it until the Lentils are tender. This should take about 15-20m. Set them aside once you are done
  5. Heat up your Vegetable Oil in a large pan, and begin frying your Onion until the pieces turn golden. Now add the Ground Beef and cook until that turns light brown. Add Salt, optionally Turmeric, bloomed Saffron, and optionally Cinnamon. Stir until the Ground Beef finishes cooking, then reduce the heat, optionally add the Raisins and dates, and stir once more. Now, set this aside for later use
  6. Pour 6 cups of water into a large nonstick pot and bring it to a boil. Add rice into the pot, and after 6-8m, once it begins to rise to the top and the water begins boiling again, check the rice is cooked by trying a grain. Finally, pour the rice into a Colander to drain it; then set it aside.
  7. Scoop 1/3 of the rice into a pot. Layer it with 1/3 of the cooked Lentils, and repeat until it’s all in there, then shape it into a pyramid. Add 3 tbsps of your bloomed Saffron to this pot. Cover the pot and cook it until the rice begins to steam; about 30-40m
  8. Transfer to a serving dish with the Ground Beef Mixture. Enjoy!

Loobia Polo

Figure 1: Loobia Polo with bread tahdig; the optimal way to eat it (Potato tahdig works too, however)

Loobia Polo is one of my favourites for sure. It’s probably a weird sounding meal to anyone looking in from the outside; with the fact it contains Green Beans stuffed with Ground Beef/Lamb typically, but I assure you it does not disappoint! It’s absolutely delectable to enjoy with some Plain or Greek Yogurt as well, as you’re about to see.

Time Needed

  • 20m Prep Time
  • 1h40m Cook Time
  • 2h Total Time

Ingredients

  • 500g Ground Beef (This can be replaced with Chicken, or Lamb Chops, or for you Vegetarians, you can just use the Green Beans all on their own)
  • 2 whole Onions
  • 500g Green Beans (the Green Beans are the main thing here; Loobia literally means bean)
  • 3 tbsps Tomato Paste (No, not sauce; Tomato Paste is much more viscous)
  • 2 tbsps Vegetable Oil
  • 1 tsp Turmeric (Spice-wise it’s always Turmeric, huh)
  • To own taste quantity of Salt
  • To own taste quantity of Pepper
  • 1/2 cup boiling water
  • 2.5 cups long-grain Basmati Rice (Basmati Rice is so nifty… I can’t ever tell it apart from regular white rice)
  • 2 tsps Salt
  • 6 cups water
  • 2 tbsps Vegetable Oil
  • 2 tbsps bloomed Saffron

Directions

  • Green Beans Stew
    1. Wash and remove both ends of all your green beans. Chop them into 1-inch tall pieces
    2. Dice the onions finely and saute them in a pan with vegetable oil until they become transparent
    3. Add your beans in with the onions and fry them together until the green beans become soft
    4. Add your meat of choice (if any) and saute until cooked
    5. Include the turmeric, and optionally some cinnamon, as well as generally spices of your liking
    6. Pour the tomato paste in
    7. Add boiling water, and combine everything, then cover to cook for about 20m
  • Rice
    1. Bring a pot of water with salt to a rapid boil
    2. Wash and soak rice the rice, then pour it into the boiling water, and maintain a boil for about 8-10m; till the grains are long and soft on the outside, and firm in the center (it’ll sound like you’re describing bananas, but stray away from those things; some people are allergic)
    3. Drain and rinse the rice to help separate the grains and bring more fluffiness into them
  • Bringing the two together!
    1. Take out a large non-stick pot and drizzle some Vegetable Oil into it
    2. With a large spatula in hand, pour about 1/3 of your rice in, with some bloomed saffron over-top, as well as on 1/2 of the green beans mixture
    3. Layer your green bean mixture and rice into the pot (rice, saffron, green bean, then more rice) in the shape of a pyramid
    4. Pour some boiling water along the sides, then cover the pot tightly with the lid wrapped in a kitchen towel, and cook for 5m on high heat until the rice begins steaming. Lower the heat and continue steaming for another 30-40m
    5. Serve it up and enjoy!

Ghormeh Sabzi

Figure 1: A big bowl of questionably delectable looking Ghormeh Sabzi… Yeah. Not the biggest fan of this.

I’ve had a shaky history with my liking for a lot of the dishes I’ve listed here, but there’s one that has managed to especially boil my blood for years and years has been Ghormeh Sabzi. It’s undoubtedly a very unique stew… thing, and as with I’m sure many children, I turned my nose away with just one good look at the stuff. I think Ghormeh Sabzi is something I’ll constantly be at conflict with myself as to whether I truly like it or not, but I must be honest I really wouldn’t mind eating some right here right now as I’m writing this. I hope you find something new out of this dish!

Time Needed

  • 45m Prep Time
  • 2h25m Cook Time
  • 3h10m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 1/4 cup Canola Oil; divided up (I wasn’t told how it’s supposed to be divided, and regrettably I’m not omniscient so you figure it out this time, reader)
  • 1 whole large Yellow Onion; finely chopped
  • 1 tsp Ground Turmeric
  • 1.5 lbs (a.k.a 0.6803 kg for you sane folk) Boneless Chuck Roast; cut into 1.5-inch (3.81cm) cubes
  • 1.5 cups finely chopped Spinach (surely you were wondering why Ghormeh Sabzi is so green, yeah?)
  • 1 cup finely chopped Green Onions (only the green parts of the Green Onions, you got it?)
  • 1/2 cup finely chopped Italian flat-leaf Parsley (that’s right we went to Italy for this one, and if History is anything to go off of, well, you should know how big of a deal that is [thanks for everyhing, Rome])
  • 1/4 cup finely chopped Cilantro
  • 1/4 cup finely chopped Chives
  • 1/4 cup finely chopped Fenugreek Leaves (No I didn’t know that was a word either)
  • 1.5 cups water; possibly more as needed
  • To own taste quantity of Pepper
  • 1 whole Juiced Lemon
  • 4 whole dried Persian Limes (a.k.a Limoo Amani; these look very different to typical Limes and I would most certainly omit these personally)
  • 15 ounces Red Kidney Beans; drained and rinsed

Directions

  1. Heat 2 tbsps of Oil in a large pot over medium-high heat. Add your onion, and stir until deeply golden brown; about 10-15m. Stir in the turmeric for 1-2m and then add the Chuck Cubes and cook until it’s fully coated in the turmeric and browned on all sides; 8-10m.
  2. Heat the remaining 2 tbsps of Oil in another pot over medium heat. Here, add in your Spinach, Green Onions, Parsley, Cilantro, Chives, and Fenugreek Leaves. Cook and stir until deep dark green; 5-10m
  3. Stir your Spinach mixture into the Onion and Chuck mixture. Pour in enough water so as to create a slurry consistency in your pot. Season this with salt and pepper, and stir in the Lemon juice. Now, reduce the heat, cover, and let this simmer until the greens soften; about 1h
  4. Pierce the dried limes with a fork, and add to your stew. Continue simmering until the chuck is tender; 30-60m. Stir in the Red Kidney Beans at least, and cook until the flavours combine; being about another 30m more. Discard the dried limes before serving… Or don’t if you’re horrible
  5. Enjoy!

Khoresh Gheimeh

Figure 1: The way I’d usually eat Gheimeh; Yellow rice and a tahdig with the khoresh poured over it.

Gheimeh is a dish which means a lot to me; even when I don’t like it very much. I have incredibly fond memories of staying at my Grandparents’ house with my younger Cousin, and stealing a few Fries fresh off the pan as my Grandma pretended to be upset with us all the while finding it to be the most adorable thing. I always loved the Fries, never the khoresh. Quite picky I know, but it’s how I liked it. What’s more is that some days, after Kindergarten, my Uncle would pick me up when my mom couldn’t and we’d go to this little Burger restaurant around the corner of my home where he’d treat me to some Fries. Over the years, I have grown a love for the flavour of Gheimeh too, and above most of these dishes, this is the one that reminds me the most of Iran, and of my family. I miss those simpler days absconding away with a handful of Fries alongside my Cousin as my Grandma cooked. I miss her a lot more than I ever got the chance to say in the end. I find it really interesting how food is one of the key things that gets passed down across generations most triumphantly. There is truth to the idea that food tells a story, and in many ways it’s what represents you. I hope you find your own scoop of bliss in what represents me.

Time Needed

  • 30m Prep Time
  • 3h Cook Time
  • 3h30m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 300g Beef or Lamb cubes (You can omit this depending on your personal objections to meat of course, but I have had the chance of trying Gheimeh without meat and it’s a lot more bland so I wouldn’t recommend it)
  • 1 cup yellow split Peas; rinsed and drained
  • 1 whole large Onion; diced
  • 2 tbsps Tomato Paste
  • 3 whole dried Limes; pierced with a fork (Please remove these before serving; some people like to serve Gheimeh with the Limes still in there it is the most vile way to ruin perfectly good Gheimeh)
  • 2 tsps Salt
  • 1/4 tsp ground Black Pepper
  • 1/4 Cinnamon (the recipe does perfectly fine without, frankly)
  • 1/2 tsp Turmeric
  • 2 tbsps Bloomed Saffron (makes you wonder what recipes use non-bloomed Saffron)
  • 3-4 cups Water
  • 2 whole large Potatoes; peeled and cut into thin slices
  • 1/2 cup Vegetable Oil (Well, really any “This goes into the pan!” Oil, but my mom typically uses Vegetable Oil)

Directions

  1. Soak yellow split peas inside 2 cups of cold water overnight, making sure to change the water every few hours
  2. Cut your meat into small chunks if you haven’t already
  3. Chop your onion finely, and add oil to a pot alongside said onion
  4. Saute the Onion until it’s golden and soft over medium heat, then add turmeric, pepper, and tomato paste
  5. Saute the tomato paste alongside the onion for an extra 4-5m over medium-high heat
  6. Add the beef chunks to the pot and ensure they brown on all sides
  7. Drain your yellow split peas and incorporate them into the pot
  8. Add 3 cups of water, cover the pot, and leave to cook for 1h
  9. Cover the pot once more and bring it to a boil, then turn down the heat and stew-simmer gently for 1h
  10. Add dried limes and leave them in for 1h (then please for the love of god don’t forget to remove them)
  11. Add salt, bloomed saffron, and (optionally) cinnamon and allow for it to thicken to your preferred consistency
  12. Cut your potatoes into thin strips if you haven’t already (or, frankly preferably, make Julienne Fries)
  13. Rinse the potatoes in a colander under cold running water, then pat them dry
  14. Heat some oil in a saucepan, and deep fry the potatoes until they’re golden brown and crispy
  15. Ladle your Gheimeh into a bowl, and top with those lovely fries from before. Serve with some fluffy rice or with thin bread
  16. Enjoy!

Dinners

Aush Reshteh

Figure 1: An overview of a bowl of Aush Reshteh; usually made for festive occasions with lots of people

Aush Restheh is most definitely not among my favourite of the Persian dishes. Most people would likely fight me on this, but eating it on occasion back in Iran, I couldn’t help but be appalled by it’s viscosity, smell, and look so much that I never got to the taste. It’s a very festive dish in our culture; due to the large quantities yielded from an average bowl of Aush, as well as the amount of time one needs to simply let it sit and brew over heat, it would be reserved only for the most special nights to be enjoyed with many, many friends and family. It isn’t something I’ve eaten in a number of years… I’d like to give it another try someday. I’m curious to see if I still feel so opposed to it.

Time Needed

  • 20m Prep Time
  • 3h40m Cook Time
  • 8h Additional Time
  • 12h Total Time

Ingredients

  • 1/3 Cup Dry Chickpeas (a.k.a Garbanzo Beans) (optional; I wouldn’t throw these in but I don’t like Aush much in general)
  • 1/3 Cup Dry Kidney Beans
  • 2 tbsps Olive Oil; divided or as needed
  • 1 large thinly sliced Red Onion
  • 1 tbsp Dried Mint (I’m so sorry in advance)
  • 2 large thinly sliced Yellow Onions (apparently yellow Onions are… A thing?)
  • 6 cloves minced garlic (or to taste… Prepare to brush your teeth very thoroughly tonight)
  • 2 tsps (tea, not table this time) Ground Turmeric
  • 6 cups Vegetable Stock
  • 3 cups packed chopped Fresh Parsley (Bleh too much Parsley; I’d recommend maybe do 2 cups instead)
  • 2 cups packed chopped Fresh Cilantro
  • 2 cups packed chopped Fresh Mint (on top of the dried mint… Bleh)
  • 3 bunches (that’s a measurement unit apparently) chopped Scallions (but only the green parts)
  • 1/2 cup dry Lentils (I know we say only 1/2 cup but you’ll notice these buggers in there)
  • 1/2lb (0.2267kg for us civilized folk) Linguine Pasta (To be most accurate you’ll find to actually use Reshteh Noodles, but that’s only if you manage to find them)
  • 1lb (0.4535kg) fresh chopped Spinach
  • 1 tbsp all-purpose Flour (or make your own very specialized-purpose Aush Reshteh Flour if you’d like; I won’t stop you)
  • 1 cup Greek Yogurt (also optional but I like Greek Yogurt and will hunt you down if you don’t include it)

Directions

  1. Transport your Chickpeas and Kidney Beans into a large container and cover with several inches of cool water. Now, simply place the container on a counter because you are now going to let this stand for 8 hours overnight.
  2. Heat 1 tbsp of Olive Oil in a skillet over medium heat, then spread your chunks of red onions in and stir. Continue stirring until the onions turns translucent; about 5m. Now, reduce the heat to low and continue stirring until your Onions caramelize into little tender golden brown chunks; about 40m. Now stir in your dried mint, and continue cooking until the Onions begin turning dark brown and crisp; about 15m. Once done, set this aside to be used later as garnish.
  3. Heat your remaining Olive Oil in a stock pot. Now, you’ll stir in the Yellow Onions, and continue doing so until the Onions have turned translucent; about 5m. Add some minced garlic in, and stir until you can smell that decidedly not-so-fantastic fragrant; about 1m. Stir in some more Chickpeas, kidney beans, and include some turmeric now; toss your pot to combine everything. Finally, pour in your Vegetable Stock, and let this soup simmer for 1 hour.
  4. Stir some Parsley, Cilantro, Mint (ew), Scallions, and Lentils into your soup. Allow this to now simmer an extra 30m. Break your noodles of choice (Linguine or Reshteh) into 3 sections and stir into the soup. Cook this at a low boil until the noodles go soft (this will take about 8-10m with Linguine)
  5. Stir 1/2 of your Spinach into the Soup until it has wilted; about 1m. Now, add in the remaining Spinach and allow this to simmer for 30 minutes, occasionally stirring some more. (Yeah, I know, lots and lots of stirring.)
  6. Stir some flour alongside 3 tbsps of the soup together inside a small bowl until smooth. Now, re-introduce the flour back into your soup, and continue to simmer it over low heat, making sure to frequently stir the pot, until it has thickened; about 30m.
  7. Finally, we eat! Serve your newly made Aush Reshteh in bowls with optional little spoons of yogurt and red onions over top. Enjoy!

Tahchin

Figure 1: Tahchin; this absolute abomination of a food that somehow turns out delicious (if not dry) every time… That is if you spare yourself the barberries you goon

What is there that I could hope to accomplish by speaking about Tahchin? My main memories of it were all during uncomfortably large Mehmoonies always strangely hosted at some Uncle or Aunt’s house where all the lights were always disorientingly warm for my little 5 year old eyes. If you want to know what Mehmoonies are, they’re Persian almost-parties featuring relatives that are distant to you, but just barely not distant enough for it to still be expected of you to know them and have memories of them from when you were 3. Key staples of Mehmoonies include somehow shiny wooden furniture, a minimum of 2 Persian rugs in every room, the confusingly pleasant smell of smoke, Uncles with either completely white hair and a comically large moustache, or no hair whatsoever, the expectancy of staying until 2:00 in the morning, several dozen goodbyes out the door and from your car when it turns out you don’t have the stomach to stay until 2:00 in the morning, and of course, a large dinner plate of incredibly dry food. Ahhh culture. Tahchin was always a welcome addition to these ensembles, of course; notoriously difficult to make as it is. I’ve always liked the crunchy layer of rice, actually; it’s quite unique. I think you may too, reader.

Time Needed

  • 20m Prep Time
  • 1h30m Cook Time
  • 1h50m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 2 cups Basmati Rice
  • 2 whole eggs
  • 1/2 cup plain Yogurt (optional but I’d recommend it; if you haven’t gotten the gist yet, Tahchin is usually pretty dry on it’s own)
  • 2 tbsps Butter
  • 1/4 cup oil (pretty much any Oil will work)
  • 4 tbsps Bloomed Saffron
  • To own taste quantity of Salt and Pepper
  • 1 whole Boneless Chicken Breast (or de-bone it yourself, you vile fellow)
  • 1 whole Onion
  • To own taste quantity of Turmeric (I’d go with quite a lot; Turmeric is delicious, what can I say?)
  • 2 tbsps Slivered Almonds (technically optional, but just generally you will make an enemy of me if you actually use these)
  • 2 tbsps Slivered Pistachios (also optional, and the same deal as with the Almonds… Please just don’t)

Directions

  1. Soak your Barberries in water for 1h (or skip this step if you’re not using Barberries like a decent member of society)
  2. Cut your onion into small chunks, and begin cooking with the Chicken, as well as 1 glass of water, salt, pepper, and turmeric on gentle heat until the water fully evaporates. Make sure to then cut the chicken into small bite-sized pieces
  3. Rinse your rice until the water runs clear. Next, pour it into a large pan filled with water, and add some salt and cook for 6-8m. It’ll begin to rise to the top and the water will begin to boil. Once this happens, check that the rice is cooked by trying a grain. The rice should be soft on the outside, yet still firm in the middle. Once this is the case, pour it into a colander to drain, and set it aside
  4. Mix your plain Yogurt, Egg yolks, and bloomed Saffron in a bowl
  5. Add 1/2 of your rice into the Yogurt mixture and begin whisking by hand
  6. Add 3 tbsps of Oil to your rice cooker or a pan
  7. Pour half of the Saffron-rice into this pan. Flatten the first layer with a spoon, and add a layer of chicken pieces, then cover the entire surface with your remaining plain rice
  8. Pour your remaining liquid Saffron over the rice. Alternatively, you could use Oil or melted butter
  9. Put your pot on medium-high heat, and cover it, leaving it to cook until the rice begins to steam. Lower the heat and cook for 1h until a crust begins to form
  10. Serve on a large plate; Tahchin can usually be cut into slices like a cake, most optimally
  11. Enjoy!

Kebab Koobideh

Figure 1: Now here’s some really good stuff! Skewers of Kebab Koobideh served with buttery rice, a grilled tomato, and some onions

This is (just slightly arguably) my favourite dish in the world. Kebab Koobideh is, in truth, quite a simple food: Skewers of Lamb or Beef pounded onto the metal rods and grilled over an open fire, then served either with thin bread or buttery rice. I like it with rice most of all, and I’ve found that adding some Sumac and a Sunny-Side up egg over it really completes it. This is one food I have the absolute fondest memories of from Iran. I’ve been to parties where we’ve had Skewers of Kebab grilled over a bonfire, and I’ve been to incredibly stylish restaurants in cities whose names I can’t even recall with such fatty, flavourful Kebab that it’s to die for much of the time. There are some smaller restaurants in Toronto that try to replicate that, but I haven’t found anything that compares to the flavour I remember from back in Iran. I don’t just say that to pump you up either, dear reader; hopefully there will come better days with a better Government over there where it’ll be open for all to safely visit once more. If that does happen, and you feel compelled to go explore the country, this is one food you’d be kidding yourself by skipping.

Time Needed

  • 30m Prep Time
  • 25m Cook Time
  • 30m Additional Time
  • 1h25m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 2 whole peeled Onions
  • 1 lb (0.453kg for you proper gentlefolk) Ground Lamb
  • 1 lb (0.453kg) Ground Beef (You can technically pick one meat over the other, but I’d highly recommend using both Lamb and Beef if possible for your palette; it’s the best way to make Koobideh Kebab)
  • 1 whole diced Green Onion
  • 2 tsps Baking Powder
  • 1 tsp Salt
  • 1 tsp Ground Black Pepper
  • 1 tsp Sumac Powder (Please, please don’t skip this it adds so much more to the flavour than you could ever imagine)
  • 1/2 tsp Ground Turmeric
  • 2 whole Eggs
  • 8 (10inch or 25.4cm) Metal Skewers
  • 2.5 cups Basmati Rice
  • 1 tsp Melted Butter (optional if you have objections, of course; this will be mixed in with the rice later)

Directions

  1. Shave your Onions with a grater into a fine mesh strainer. Gently press the Onion pulp into the strainer to remove the juices. Blanket your Onion pulp in a cheesecloth, and gently press until the flow of juices stop
  2. Place your lamb and beef into a pot. Add green onions and the onion pulp, and mix until thoroughly combined. Mix in some baking powder, salt, pepper, sumac, and turmeric. Add your eggs and mix those thoroughly. Cover the pot and place in the fridge for about 30m
  3. Preheat an outdoor grill to medium heat and lightly oil the grate (If you’re using a charcoal grill, then medium heat roughly translates to why-are-you-doing-this-to-yourself degrees Celsius)
  4. Mold the meat mixture onto some skewers, ensuring there remains some space on the ends to grab the Skewers by, of course. The meat should have small little teeth or acute gaps in it roughly every 3cm or so
  5. Get cooking! Make sure to rotate the Skewers about every minute, and keep going until the meat is nicely browned on all sides and no longer pink in the center; about 25m
  6. Serve with some Basmati Rice or even just some thin bread (I emphasize the need for the bread to be thin because it’s important that it absorbs the juices of the meat)
  7. Optionally also add some Onions to the side to eat alongside the Kebab, or even a runny Sunny-side Up egg over the rice
  8. Enjoy!

Kotlet

Figure 1: Some lovely Oily little Kotlet patties alongside Broccoli’s and what looks like Tater Tots.

Kotlet is most certainly a comfort food of mine. This must’ve been one of the first foods I ever ate, and I’m still an avid fan of it. They’re this little potato and meat patties and they’re moist but structured, they contain all my favourite flavours, and they can easily be served with Rice, as patties to a sandwich, or even all on their own on platters in parties. (Though do note they’re typically too messy to be ’finger food’)

Time Needed

  • 15m Prep Time
  • 45m Cook Time
  • 1h Total Time

Ingredients

  • 300g Ground Beef (You can omit this if you’d like, but I find it does make quite a noticeable difference in the texture)
  • 2 whole medium Potatoes
  • 1 whole Egg
  • 1 whole large Onion
  • 2 tbsps All-Purpose Flour (or alternatively, Chickpea Flour)
  • 1 tsp Salt
  • 1/4 tsp Black Pepper
  • 1/2 tsp Turmeric

Directions

  1. Grate your Onion into a bowl with a hand-grater. Similarly to the Kebab, squeeze the juices out of this Onion Pulp by wrapping it in a cheesecloth and lightly squeezing
  2. Grate your Potatoes into a pulp as well, and add them into the bowl
  3. Finally, add your Ground Beef, Flour, Egg, Salt, Pepper, and Turmeric into the mixture
  4. Knead this until the ingredients are fully incorporated with one another (if you feel the mixture isn’t firm enough to hold it’s shape, add more Flour. Be sure to not over-mix or knead the mixture)
  5. Take a palm-sized ball of the mixture and flatten it into a patty with your hands
  6. Preheat a frying pan with vegetable oil, and add the Kotlet patties. Fry them over medium-low heat until they turn crispy and golden brown. Make sure to flip them and fry the other side as well until both sides are thoroughly crispy. Make as many patties as you can with the quantity of the mixture you made. Have a plate beside you to set Kotlets aside on once they’re ready
  7. Serve the Kotlets with tomatoes, pickles, and bread, or simply with some White or Basmati Rice
  8. Enjoy!

Sholezard

Figure 1: A bowl of Sholezard topped with various… Things as well as a bouquet of Cinnamon off to the side for added stylishness.

Sholezard is one of few recipes in this Cookbook which I have little experience with. It’s one I have faint memories of seeing my relatives eat in early mornings in cars during road trips through the mountain clearings, but it’s not one I ever tasted for myself; likely because I found the appearance to be off-putting and that matters an unfortunate amount in relation to food when you’re young. It’s a rice pudding which heavily features the use of Saffron and so it is my belief that you’ll find it to be a very rich kind of sweet. A sort of rock candy kind of sweet, perhaps; though that’s just my brain’s way of interpreting it. Let’s find out together, shall we my most enlightened reader?

Time Needed

  • 1h Prep Time
  • 2h Cook Time
  • 3h Total Time

Ingredients

  • 1 cup Rice (I bet you weren’t expecting to see that in a rice pudding)
  • 6 cups Water
  • 2 cups Sugar
  • 1/4 cup Almond Slices
  • 50g Butter
  • 1/3 cup Rosewater (We really do love our Rosewater)
  • 1/4 cup grounded and brewed Saffron
  • 1/4 tsp ground Cardamom (Optional; I don’t believe I’ve had Cardamom before but it looks like it may be worth keeping in)
  • To own taste quantity of Cinnamon, Pistachio slices, and Almond

Directions

  1. The night before you begin coking the Sholezard, leave some rice in room temperature water to soak
  2. Pour the rice as well as the water it’s been soaking in into a pot, and bring it to a boil. Once a boil is reached, lower the heat so as to soften the rice
  3. Leave the rice in the pot for 1h and then add Sugar
  4. Lower the heat, and add Butter, Saffron, as well as the Cardamoms. Begin stirring from here on out
  5. Continue doing this for 2-3h until it has reached the consistency you desire. Then, add Rosewater and more Cardamom. Now, leave it to cook for a final 5m then serve
  6. Add some sliced Almonds as garnish (optional)
  7. Enjoy!

Halva

Figure 1: A hearty decorated plate of Halva with an inward pattern and a little flower-garnish in the middle

Halva is the single food in this menu which my opinion has changed about in the most jarring manner. It’s a thick, sweet sort of dessert which I found to be rather sickeningly sweet when I was younger, but I absolutely love Halva alongside a cup of tea nowadays. It’s really a perfect representation of Persian desserts I’d say. Sweet, savoury, and perfect with a cup of tea.

Time Needed

  • 10m Prep Time
  • 10m Cook Time
  • 1h Additional Time
  • 1h20m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 1 cup White Sugar (this may somehow be the first recipe to specify what kind of sugar should be used)
  • 1/2 cup Water
  • 1/4 cup Rosewater
  • 3 threads Saffron
  • 1 cup Unsalted Butter (use Salted Butter at your own risk; that’s dangerous stuff you’re dealing with)
  • 1.5 cups All-Purpose Flour

Directions

  1. Pour your Sugar and Water into a Saucepan and bring them to a boil, making sure to constantly stir until the sugar is dissolved; about 5m
  2. Stir the Rosewater and Saffron into the Saucepan, and cover it as you turn off the heat
  3. Melt some butter in another large Saucepan over low heat; stir some flour into it until the mixture turns into a smooth paste. Continue cooking while stirring until the paste turns golden; about 5-10m
  4. Slowly whisk some Sugar into the paste until it turns smooth. Remove the Saucepan from heat
  5. Spread this newly made Halva evenly onto a plate/platter. You can optionally make a pattern by pressing little graves into the surface of the Halva with the back of a spoon or butter knife. Cover it with Plastic Wrap and refrigerate until it’s set; at least 1h
  6. Enjoy! (with a cup of black Tea)

Ranginak

Figure 1: A small piece of Ranginak; most commonly found on display in Persian bakeries

Ranginak is a dessert I’ve never personally tasted before, but I have seen it a lot on display in little Bakeries every now and then. I always preferred the fudge chocolate things instead, and they were always a viable alternative so I never got the chance to taste Ranginaks. I don’t imagine they’d be the tastiest since I really dislike Dates but it’s also been a long time since I’ve had them.

Time Needed

  • 15m Prep Time
  • 15m Cook Time
  • 30m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 4 cups pitted Mejdool Dates (about 30 of them, therefore; if you’ve never had Dates before… I’m so sorry in advance)
  • 1.5 cups Walnuts; broken into large pieces
  • 1 cup Unsalted Butter
  • 1.75 cups All-Purpose-Flour
  • 1/2 cup Powdered Sugar
  • 3/4 tsp Ground Cinnamon
  • 1/2 tsp Ground Cardamom
  • 1 cup Pistachios

Directions

  1. Apply butter to a 10-inch Pie Plate and line it with 3 parchment paper strips so as to extend over the edge of the pan. Be sure to apply Butter in between the strips
  2. Heat a Cast-Iron Skillet at medium heat and toast some Walnuts for about 5m. Set it aside to cool
  3. Pack the pitted Dates with Walnut pieces and arrange it in the Pie Plate. Press down on the soft dates to fill any large gaps
  4. Melt butter in the Skillet ad medium heat, and add flour whilst toasting it lightly. Stir continuously for about 15-20m until the flour takes on a spreadable consistency
  5. Pour the flour-butter mixture immediately over your Dates. Using a spatula, spread it evenly and pack it into the date layer
  6. Combine Powdered Sugar, Ground Cinnamon, Ground Cardamom, and sift it evenly over the dough mixture
  7. Sprinkle Ground Pistachios in an even layer over the Sugar and press it down lightly
  8. Cover, and set it overnight at room temperature. If desired, you can sift Powdered Sugar and/or cocoa powder in decorative patterns over the Pistachio Layer
  9. Run a knife around the dessert to loosen it from the pan. Then, use the parchment paper to transfer it to a cutting board and cut it into pieces
  10. Enjoy!

Khagineh

Figure 1: A plate of Khagineh made in the decidedly less Tabrizi way. Looks like an Apple Pie, doesn’t it?

Khagineh is one of few dishes I have absolutely no experience with inside this Cookbook. It’s a dish that originated in Azerbaijan and is more popular amongst the folk in Tabriz, which somewhat explains my unfamiliarity with it since I’m a hip and cool Tehran fellow myself. Just from the Ingredients list and pictures it looks mighty delicious, however!

Time Needed

  • 15m Prep Time
  • 15m Cook Time
  • 30m Total Time

Ingredients

  • 5 whole Eggs
  • 6 tbsps Flour
  • 5 tbsps Yogurt (Tip: Next time you’re enjoying a bag of regular flavoured Potato Chips, try using plain or Greek Yogurt as a dip it’s so good I don’t know why that’s surprisingly obscure)
  • 2 cups Sugar
  • 1 soupspoon (Soupspoon???) Baking Powder
  • To own taste quantity of Saffron

Directions

  1. Pour your eggs into a bowl and add the Yogurt. Stir them until they’re completely mixed, then add Baking Powder and Flour, and continue stirring
  2. Add oil to a pot, and once the Oil goes hot, lower the heat. Pour the ingredients from the bowl into the pot and cover it for 20m. Ond side of the Khagineh must be fried during this time, so flip it to fry the other side. It may be easier to use a heat diffuser for this.
  3. Take it out of the pot, cut into pieces, and serve with some Syrup of your choice
  4. Enjoy!
